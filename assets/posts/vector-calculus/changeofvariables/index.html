Introduction

Although the typical cartesian coordinate system is extremeley useful, there are a few other coordinate systems that prove better to describe and integrate over certain surfaces. But before looking at specific examples, let's understand how exactly to utilize this knowledge.

Suppose we are given a surface \(A\), described to us using coordinate system \(X\) such that \( \int_A \text{d}A = \int_{x=a}^b \text{d} x \). But, we want to integrate over \(A\) in coordinate system \(Y\). We know the relation between the coordinate systems, and can describe the way to get the \(Y\)-coordinates of a point from its \(X\)-coordinates as a function \(\Phi\). So, we can describe a surface \(B\) in \(Y\)-coordinates such that \(\Phi(B) = A\), and let \( (f(a),f(b)) \) reflect \(B\)'s boundary in \(Y\)-coordinates. It may seem like the next step is as simple as following

$$ \int_{A} \text{d}A = \int_{x=a}^b \text{d} x = \int_{y=f(a)}^{f(b)} \Phi(y) \text{d}y = \int_{B} \text{d}B $$

but it isn't quite so straightforward. Changing coordinates (or changing variables) introduces complications into integration because it turns out that it has the potential to warp volumes. So a volume might "look" bigger in \(Y\)-coordinates than it does in \(X\) coordinates, and we need to take that into account when integrating. The factor by which the change of variables warps volumes is measured by the determinant of the jacobian \(\Phi^\prime(y)\), so we can now correctly write

$$ \int_{A} \text{d}A = \int_{x=a}^b \text{d} x = \int_{y=f(a)}^{f(b)} |\Phi(y)^\prime| \Phi(y) \text{d}y = \int_{B} \text{d}B $$

The proof that \(\Phi^\prime\) does the job for us requires some more background, but ultimately it is a direct consequence of the Curl Theorem, which is in turn a special case of Stokes' Theorem.
